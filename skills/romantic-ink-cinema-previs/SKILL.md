# Romantic Ink Cinema Previs

## Overview

Use this skill when the goal is to visualize emotion as movement, light, space, and rhythm. This is not a character-design skill. It converts relationship feeling into camera logic, body timing, energy behavior, density control, and SFX structure.

## Core Principle

Always translate emotion into five systems:

- camera choreography
- body rhythm
- energy interaction
- spatial tension and release
- negative-space pacing

Do not focus on facial features, wardrobe, anatomy detail, or realistic material description unless the user explicitly requires them. Prioritize silhouette, timing, spacing, light behavior, and emotional pressure.

## When To Use

Use this skill when the user asks for any of the following:

- a romantic cinematic previs prompt
- a couple interaction sequence with stylized energy or ink light
- a shot-by-shot emotional motion design
- a 10–15 second visual beat breakdown
- a prompt for image-to-video, text-to-video, or previs generation where emotion must be shown through motion rather than dialogue
- a motion-language system for romance, longing, tenderness, tension, or abstract intimacy

Do not use this skill for:

- realistic screenplay dialogue writing
- detailed fashion, face, or anatomy prompting
- literal storyboard art direction focused on props or sets only
- generic “make it cinematic” prompts with no emotional choreography

## Output Modes

Choose the lightest format that matches the request:

1. **Beat Breakdown**
   Use when the user wants timed segments like `0:00–0:02`, `0:02–0:04`.
2. **Single Prompt**
   Use when the user wants one compact generative prompt.
3. **Skill Format / System Format**
   Use when the user asks to turn a method into a reusable framework, prompt template, or skill package.

If the user does not specify a format, default to **Beat Breakdown** for short-form motion concepts.

## Narrative Motion Formula

Default emotional structure:

1. **Approach**
   Distance narrows, gestures remain restrained, camera follows gently, energy begins to appear.
2. **Resonance**
   Motion starts to mirror, energy exchanges, camera begins to orbit or drift sideways.
3. **Accumulation**
   Energy density increases, camera push-pull grows stronger, emotional pressure thickens.
4. **Release**
   Extreme proximity or lens collision, decisive emotional action, energy floods the frame.
5. **Afterglow**
   Light dissolves, density falls away, frame returns to breath and negative space.

Useful language:

- narrowing distance
- mirrored rhythm
- intertwined motion
- stacking light structures
- full-frame flood
- dissolving atmosphere

## Visual Language Rules

### 1. Describe relation, not decoration

Prefer:

- silhouette edges
- hand rhythm
- shoulder turn
- drifting particles
- contact points
- energy adherence
- spatial compression

Avoid over-describing:

- eyes, lips, lashes
- textile detail
- realistic skin texture
- exact costume rendering

### 2. Camera must participate in emotion

The camera is never a passive observer. It should be:

- pulled by attraction
- pushed away by emotional force
- compressed by proximity
- destabilized by contact
- soothed by afterglow

Useful camera phrasing:

- camera retreats under pressure
- lens drifts into intimacy
- camera snaps back to forward angle
- orbiting motion gathers emotional density
- extreme push-in collides with the moment

### 3. Energy must feel physical

Never write just “glow” or “light” without behavior.

Energy should:

- adhere to skin
- linger in the air
- bleed along silhouette edges
- contract inward
- accumulate between bodies
- scatter, then reassemble
- wrap around bodies as halos or trails

### 4. Rhythm should feel choreographed like music

Treat each beat as a phrase. In short-form romance previs, a clean default is a new rhythmic function every ~2 seconds.

Recommended pacing map:

- `0–2s`: establish relationship field
- `2–4s`: first contact or contact preparation
- `4–6s`: emotional warming
- `6–8s`: resonance and orbit
- `8–10s`: density build
- `10–12s`: threshold approach
- `12–13.5s`: suspended stillness
- `13.5–15s`: release

## Camera Choreography Library

### Approach Stage

- low-angle tracking
- soft side pan
- slow retreat
- drifting close shot

### Warming / Resonance

- snap-back motion
- push-pull camera
- orbit rotation
- perspective distortion

### Release Stage

- extreme frontal close shot
- ultra-wide distortion
- aggressive push-in
- lens-collision framing

## Energy System

### Romantic Variant

Forms:

- fingertip glow
- pale ink light
- soft halos
- breathing particles
- warm diffusion

Behavior:

- floating
- slow merging
- soft outward ripples
- emotional adhesion
- inward convergence before release

### Heavier / Aggressive Variant

Forms:

- dense black ink
- heavy brush streaks
- explosive calligraphy masses
- violent ink arcs

Behavior:

- weight
- pressure
- speed
- rupture
- frame invasion

If the user does not specify, default to the **Romantic Variant** for tenderness and intimacy.

## SFX Logic

SFX is not decoration. It should reinforce:

- movement weight
- contact intimacy
- spatial pressure
- breath pacing
- energy transformation

Romantic palette:

- soft piano overtones
- gentle strings
- breathing
- fingertip contact
- fabric rustle
- ambient airflow
- light ripple textures

When writing timed beats, include one concise SFX line per beat.

## Acceptance Criteria

Treat the skill as passing only when all of the following are true:

- **Trigger fit**: it clearly applies to requests about romantic ink-light previs, emotional shot-by-shot motion, couple choreography, or cinematic prompts where emotion must be visualized through movement instead of appearance detail.
- **Method fidelity**: the response preserves the core translation layer of this skill: camera choreography, body rhythm, energy interaction, spatial tension/release, and negative-space pacing.
- **Narrative arc integrity**: unless the user explicitly overrides it, the output should reflect the full emotional motion logic of `Approach -> Resonance -> Accumulation -> Release -> Afterglow`.
- **Energy physics**: light or ink is described as behavior with inertia and residue, not as static decoration. It should adhere, linger, bleed, contract, wrap, scatter, merge, or flood.
- **Camera participation**: the camera must act as part of the emotion, not a neutral observer. The output should show how the lens is pulled, pushed, compressed, snapped, orbited, or stilled by emotional pressure.
- **Format stability**: if the user asks for a timed previs breakdown, each beat should reliably include `Lens`, `Camera`, `Character Motion`, `Energy Interaction`, `Spatial Emotion`, `Visual Density`, and `SFX`, or a prose variant that still covers those seven layers.
- **Anti-drift guardrail**: the output should not collapse into face details, wardrobe rendering, anatomy description, or generic cinematic adjectives with no motion logic.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Identify the emotional engine

Decide what is being visualized:

- approach
- hesitation
- tenderness
- pull
- surrender
- reunion
- rupture

### Step 2: Choose a density curve

State whether the sequence is:

- low-density and airy
- steadily accumulating
- abrupt and explosive
- soft afterglow dominant

### Step 3: Build the motion in beats

For each beat, define:

- Lens
- Camera
- Character Motion
- Energy Interaction
- Spatial Emotion
- Visual Density
- SFX

You do not need to expose every label if the user wants a cinematic prose format, but you must think through all seven.

### Step 4: Keep the language abstract but filmable

The scene should feel directable. The prompt must describe:

- where the camera is
- how bodies move
- how energy behaves
- how distance changes
- how the frame fills or clears

## Style Guardrails

- Keep the prose precise and cinematic, not purple or overwritten.
- Use clean, tactile verbs: drift, narrow, orbit, merge, adhere, flood, dissolve.
- Avoid plot-heavy storytelling unless explicitly requested.
- Prefer motion and energy verbs over adjective stacking.
- End strong: the final beat should resolve either in flood, silence, or negative-space calm.
